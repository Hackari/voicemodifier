The Speed Limit of Sound

Since all audio recordings are just complex stacks of sine waves, what happens if we want to play a melody 2x faster?

The simplest way a computer can do this is by taking the wave and literally squishing it in half to play it in half the time.

Normal Melody (1x Speed)
Squished Melody (2x Speed)

But there is a catch: Squishing a wave means the wave cycles happen twice as fast. A faster cycle means a higher frequency. A higher frequency means a higher pitch.

The Fix: Phase Vocoding

The Chipmunk Effect happens because naïvely changing playback speed also compresses the wave cycles, dragging pitch along for the ride. A Phase Vocoder breaks that link — it changes how fast frames play out without changing which frequencies are in each frame.

① Analysis Frames

The input is sliced into short, overlapping windows (~2048 samples). Each frame is multiplied by a Hann window — a bell-shaped curve that smoothly tapers to zero at both edges. Without this, sharp discontinuities at the frame boundaries would inject false high-frequency energy into every FFT bin.

The analysis hop Ha controls how often we take a new slice. Consecutive frames overlap by 75%, ensuring smooth reconstruction later.

② FFT — Into Frequency Space

A Fast Fourier Transform converts each windowed frame into a list of complex numbers — one per frequency bin. Each complex number encodes two pieces of information: its magnitude (how loud that frequency is) and its phase (exactly where in its cycle the sine is right now).

This is the key separation. Time and pitch are now independent quantities. We have unlocked the ability to change one without touching the other.

③ Phase Δ — Finding True Frequency

This is the heart of the algorithm. For each frequency bin, we compare the actual phase advance between two frames against the expected advance that bin's center frequency would predict. The difference — Δerr — reveals the bin's true instantaneous frequency.

The synthesis phase is then advanced by this true frequency for each output hop Hs. Because we're tracking true frequency rather than just copying phases, the pitch is exactly preserved no matter how far apart we place the output frames.

④ IFFT + Overlap-Add

An Inverse FFT converts the phase-corrected spectrum back into a short time-domain frame. The frame is multiplied by the Hann window again, then added to the output at its new position — the synthesis hop Hs.

Placing frames further apart (Hs > Ha) produces a slower result; closer together (Hs < Ha) produces a faster one. Because phase was handled correctly, frequencies stay exactly the same regardless. This is Weighted Overlap-Add — and it's why there are no audible glitches at the boundaries.
