How do we make this faster?

Since all audio recordings are just complex stacks of sine (and cosine) waves, what happens if we want to play a melody 2x faster?

The simplest way a computer can do this is by taking the wave and literally squishing it in half to play it in half the time.

Normal Melody (1x Speed)
Squished Melody (2x Speed)

But there is a catch: Squishing a wave means the wave cycles happen twice as fast. A faster cycle means a higher frequency. A higher frequency means a higher pitch.

The Fix: Phase Vocoding

You cannot naïvely change playback speed without changing pitch. The Phase Vocoder solves this:

The next section is a deep dive into how the algorithm works.

① Slow Down by Inserting Gaps

The most obvious approach: cut the signal into short chunks, then insert a silence gap of equal length between each one. Every chunk plays at its original pitch; the total duration doubles. In theory, ½-speed without changing pitch.

In practice, this fails completely. At every chunk-gap and gap-chunk boundary the signal jumps discontinuously from some non-zero amplitude to zero. Even for very small chunks, the ear hears each jump as a sharp click. A rapid sequence of clicks becomes a harsh buzz that completely drowns out the underlying sound. And if you want to speed up rather than slow down, you'd have to discard chunks — permanently losing information.

② Filling the Gap — Naive Interpolation

The clicks came from abrupt jumps to silence. The natural fix: instead of inserting silence, connect the end of one chunk to the start of the next by interpolation — for example, a straight line between the two endpoints.

This removes the jump-to-zero discontinuity, but introduces a different problem. A straight line is not a sine wave. Analysed in the frequency domain, it injects spurious harmonics that were never in the original signal — the filled region has entirely the wrong frequency content. The result sounds smeared and unnatural. And there are still subtle discontinuities in the waveform's slope at the junction points, which the ear is very sensitive to.

③ Overlapping Frames + The Hann Window

The real insight: forget gaps entirely. Take frames that overlap by 75%, and when placing them at the output, simply add them together. Every point in the output is covered by multiple frames — no gaps, no need to interpolate anything from scratch.

But overlapping raw frames creates amplitude bumps wherever frames pile up. The fix: multiply every frame by a Hann window — a bell-shaped function that tapers smoothly to exactly zero at both ends. With 75% overlap, the sum of squared window values, ∑w², is nearly constant everywhere. This means we can divide it out and recover a perfectly flat amplitude — no bumps, no pops, no injected harmonics. We now have a robust way to place audio frames at any output position.

The ratio of the overlap (also know as the Analysis Hop) to the window length affects the reconstructed signal greatly. A greater overlap will result in more frames, allowing the signal to be modified and reproduced with more granularity. As expected, this comes with the cost of computation.

④ FFT — Extracting Amplitude and Phase

Now each overlapping frame is processed by a Fast Fourier Transform, which decomposes the frame into its constituent waves. For a frame of N samples, the FFT returns N complex numbers — one per frequency bin. Each complex number encodes exactly two things: its magnitude (the height of the bar: how loud that frequency is) and its phase (the clock angle: exactly where in its cycle that frequency's sine wave is sitting right now).

This separation is the key. Every component of the sound — every partial — now has an exact, measurable "clock position" in its cycle. We have isolated frequency from time. At this point, we can now start to shift our frames around.

⑤ The Phase Difference — Measuring True Frequency

Each frequency has a nominal centre frequency, which predicts exactly how much the phase should advance between two consecutive frames, which is the analysis hop multiplied by the frequency.

When we look at the next frame, we see that the actual phase change is different from the predicted phase change. If the phase has moved more than expected, then we know our frequency is higher than expected. Conversely, if the phase is behind, then our frequency is lower than expected.

The tiny gap between the expected and actual phase advance allows us to determine the true instantaneous frequency at each time. We now create new frames, using the true instantaneous frequency multiplied by the Synthesis Hop to lengthen or shorten parts of sound while keeping the the pitch the same. If the Synthesis Hop is larger than the Analysis Hop, then the frames would be further apart meaning the sound is slowed down, and vice versa.

⑥ IFFT, Re-window, and Overlap-Add

We now use the created frames to reconstruct the sound. The Inverse FFT is used to convert our frames in the frequency domain back into the time domain.

We now place this output along the time axis, with each frame having a distance of the previously used Synthesis Hop. Since the overlapping frames will once again create amplitude bumps, we apply the Hann Window again to smoothen the sound. Adding back the waves together then reconstructs the modified sound!
